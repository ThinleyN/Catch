Catch Me Who Can is an investigation into various modes of organization, access, and retrieval within image data banks. Questioning how different mechanisms of order influence our reading and understating of images. How certain meanings emerge, change, and stretch when algorithms act upon images and when they are framed in pages of a book. The project explores nearly one thousand protest images, collected and grouped around COCO objects. COCO (common objects in context) is a large-scale training database widely used in computer vision. It contains images of ‘’91 object types that are meant to be easily recognizable by a 4-year-old’’. However, in this new assembly, objects are extracted from their previous narrative, they are invited to become fragments in telling the stories of protests and struggles. The installation highlights a model train pulling a Wi-Fi camera. Following its route, a neural network model classifies and labels objects in real-time. These labels are used by an algorithm to retrieve images from corresponding categories. The train signals across the layout create a time-based setting for moments of pause and flow. While the train discloses images in brief flashes, the accompanying book captures them alongside extended notes, addressing the connection between language and the use of captions in describing images.
A long while is an experimental video work that employs an editing methodology based on the idea of reclaiming leap seconds established by our institutionalized regulation of time. The precision of modern time calculation has led to a discrepancy in measured values. Hence, a leap second needs to be periodically added, functioning as a suspended moment, while we wait for the earth to catch up. As a reflection of this, the composition, decomposition, and re-composition of the video’s narrative is performed by a metronome that follows a 37 degree/second subtractive structure. A decolonial re-narration unfolds as the artists appropriate the two characters from Hans Holbein painting “The Ambassadors”. They impersonate a journey through an atemporal ocean, subjected to the monotony of sea travel and a heavy feeling of boredom. The work ponders on the weight placed by western society on the precision of time keeping to sustain efficiency and productivity.
Hasan Kachal, a symbolic lazy character from an Iranian Folk tale, who spends most of his time laying down in bed. As a solution his mother formulates a plan to trick him out of the house by laying a trail of apples that forces him to experience the outside world. In this digital contemporary version, he is the Flâneur, wandering around the town with apples. He initially explores the city as a tourist to have a clear picture of the main sites, proceeding to physically leave an apple in situ and tagging them in Google Maps. Through this action he manages to modify the online experience of public space. His selection of tourist attractions in Bremen now include “apple” in their identity, showing up in queries and feeding from the interactions/ratings/ commments of users. In this journey, Hasan Kachal becomes a virtual urban hacktivist. The apples turn into imaginary places and tools against hierarchical regulations imposed by Google Maps. Through the process of sliding the rules back and forth he defines an imaginary online geography. The following is a series of documentation from the verification process.
A chapter of an ongoing project “Through binary Shath” Being in an interlanguage sequential system the errors and manipulation occurring in the process of translation, cannot simply and merely be interpreted as a misconception, but rather a text generator improvisation via a translation machine. In an extreme situation, the newly generated text could resemble Shathiyat or lettrist poetry. The concept considered as a “lost meaning” is actually a fluid reproduction of an improviser source. This source has a completely different perception and cognition from the early one and this path could be ironically bizarre. A python script utilizes google translate engine for translating between 103 supported languages, the output of each translation in each step is always the input for the next round and in the whole time the English translation is displayed above the new translated sentence. The titles are the first input sentences which here are the six verses of the Unbelievers Surah of Quran generating 6 short narratives recorded in a video format.
